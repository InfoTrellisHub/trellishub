Choosing the Right Paint Finish for Every Room

📅 28 May 2025 ⏱ 5 min read ✍️ ProFix Painting Team

Choosing the right colour is the part most people think about — but the finish is what determines how a room actually looks and how long it lasts. A beautiful colour in the wrong finish can make walls look patchy, make imperfections stand out, or leave surfaces impossible to clean in rooms that need it most.

Here's a room-by-room guide to selecting the right sheen level, and the reasoning behind each recommendation.

Understanding the four main finish levels

Flat / Matt

No sheen

Absorbs light rather than reflecting it. Hides surface imperfections exceptionally well. Not washable — marks must be painted over, not wiped off.

Best for: Low-traffic areas, ceilings
Eggshell / Satin

Low to medium sheen

Slight sheen that gives walls depth and warmth. More durable than matt and can handle light cleaning with a damp cloth. Hides most imperfections.

Best for: Living rooms, bedrooms, hallways
Semi-Gloss

Noticeable sheen

Reflects light clearly, giving a clean and bright appearance. Very durable and easy to wipe clean. Shows surface imperfections more readily.

Best for: Kitchens, bathrooms, trim, doors
Gloss

High shine

Maximum durability and moisture resistance. Highly reflective — even small surface flaws are visible. Requires excellent prep and a skilled painter to apply well.

Best for: Doors, window frames, cabinetry

The rule of thumb: The higher the traffic or moisture in a room, the higher the sheen level you need. A matt finish in a kitchen will look grimy within months; a high-gloss finish in a bedroom will make every brush mark visible.

Room-by-room recommendations

Living room
Eggshell or satin. These finishes give depth and warmth to walls that are seen in natural and artificial light. They handle the occasional scuff or fingerprint from furniture moving.
Master bedroom
Matt or eggshell. Low-traffic, low-humidity — a matt finish creates a calm, sophisticated look and hides the minor imperfections common in older plaster walls.
Children's bedroom
Satin or semi-gloss. Kids' rooms need washable walls. Satin handles repeated cleaning without losing its appearance. Semi-gloss is even more durable if the room sees significant scuffing.
Kitchen
Semi-gloss. Kitchens produce steam, grease, and splashes. Semi-gloss resists moisture and can be wiped clean repeatedly without the finish degrading.
Bathroom
Semi-gloss or gloss. The highest-humidity room in the house. Semi-gloss is the minimum — gloss is preferable for shower surrounds and any area with direct water contact.
Hallways & passages
Satin. High-traffic zones need durability. Satin hides minor scuffs while being wipeable — the practical middle ground for areas that see daily wear.
Ceilings
Flat / matt. Ceilings are almost always painted flat. A sheen on a ceiling catches light and makes every imperfection — sanding marks, joint lines — glaringly obvious.
Doors & trim
Semi-gloss or gloss. Trim and doors take constant handling. A gloss finish looks clean and sharp, wipes down easily, and the contrast between a satin wall and gloss trim is a classic finish that never dates.

One mistake most DIY painters make

Using the same finish on walls and ceilings. A wall in eggshell with a ceiling in flat (white) immediately reads as intentional and professional. Painting both in the same finish with the same colour makes the room feel flat and box-like. The finish contrast is as important as the colour contrast.

Does prep work matter more than the paint?

Yes — especially if you're using a higher sheen finish. A semi-gloss or gloss finish will faithfully reflect every crack, uneven patch, or poorly sanded joint on the wall surface. The higher the sheen, the more thorough the prep needs to be: filling, sanding, priming, and a final sand before the finish coat.

At ProFix, our painters spend as much time on preparation as they do on painting itself. This is why our finishes look sharp and last for years — not months.

Avoid matching paint in-store to an old chip. Paint colour shifts significantly as it dries, and after years on a wall it has often faded. For touch-ups or colour matching, always test a small patch on the wall in natural daylight before committing to a full coat.
