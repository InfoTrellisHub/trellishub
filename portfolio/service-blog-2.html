🚿 Geyser & Hot Water

Geyser Repair or Replace? What South African Homeowners Need to Know

📅 14 April 2025 ⏱ 6 min read ✍️ ProFix Plumbing Team

Your geyser has stopped producing hot water — or worse, it's leaking from the bottom. Now the question every homeowner faces: do you repair it, or replace it? The wrong call can cost you significantly, either through a costly repair on a geyser that fails again within a year, or an unnecessary replacement when a simple fix would have done the job.

Here's a practical framework to help you make the right decision — and what to expect from each option.

First: understand what's likely wrong

Most geyser failures fall into a handful of categories, each with different repair-versus-replace implications:

  • Element failure — the heating element has burned out (common, straightforward to replace)
  • Thermostat failure — the geyser runs but water doesn't reach the right temperature
  • Pressure control valve (PCV) or temperature/pressure relief (TPR) valve failure — water drips from the relief valve pipe outside the house
  • Tank corrosion and internal rust — discoloured or rust-smelling hot water; usually means the sacrificial anode has been depleted
  • Tank rupture or seam failure — active leak from the body of the geyser itself

Important: A leaking TPR valve doesn't always mean the geyser needs replacing — it may simply be doing its job by relieving excess pressure. Have a plumber assess before assuming the worst.

Repair vs. replace: the decision framework

✅ Lean towards repair when…

  • The geyser is under 8 years old
  • The fault is an element, thermostat, or valve (not the tank)
  • The repair cost is less than 40% of a replacement
  • The tank shows no signs of corrosion or rust
  • Hot water was previously consistent and adequate

⚠️ Lean towards replacement when…

  • The geyser is 10+ years old
  • The tank itself is leaking (cannot be repaired)
  • Hot water has an orange or rust tinge
  • This is the second or third failure in two years
  • You want to upgrade to a heat pump or solar geyser

Cost comparison

The table below gives typical cost ranges for common geyser interventions in Gauteng. Prices vary by geyser size, brand, and access difficulty.

Intervention Typical Cost Lifespan Impact Verdict
Element replacement R800 – R1,500 3–5 years if tank is sound Repair
Thermostat replacement R600 – R1,200 Several years Repair
TPR or PCV valve replacement R500 – R900 Long-term if tank is sound Repair
Full geyser replacement (150L) R6,000 – R10,000 8–12 years Replace
Heat pump upgrade R18,000 – R28,000 15+ years, ~60% energy saving Replace (upgrade)

The age rule

Most traditional electric storage geysers in South Africa have a realistic lifespan of 8–12 years with proper maintenance (including anode rod replacement every 3–5 years). After the 10-year mark, even a successful repair is likely to be followed by another failure within 1–2 years as other components reach end-of-life.

Our rule of thumb: if the repair cost exceeds 40% of the replacement cost and the geyser is over 8 years old, replacement is the better long-term investment.

Is now the time to upgrade to a heat pump?

If you're already replacing a geyser, it's worth considering a heat pump or solar geyser at the same time. Replacing a failed geyser with another standard electric unit is the cheapest option upfront — but in Eskom's pricing environment, a heat pump typically pays for the price difference within 3–4 years through electricity savings. The installation disruption is the same whether you install a standard geyser or a heat pump.

Never attempt a DIY geyser repair. Geysers operate under mains pressure with high-temperature water. In South Africa, all geyser installations and replacements must comply with SANS 10254 and be signed off by a licensed plumber. An uncertified repair can void your home insurance.
