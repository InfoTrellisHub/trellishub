5 Signs You Should See a Dentist This Week

📅 12 June 2025 ⏱ 5 min read ✍️ Dr. Sarah van der Berg

Most dental problems don't announce themselves with sudden, dramatic pain. They start quietly — a slight sensitivity here, a little tenderness there — and it's easy to brush them off as nothing serious. But early dental problems caught at this stage are almost always simpler and cheaper to treat. Left untreated, the same problem can progress into something that requires root canal treatment, tooth extraction, or even bone grafts.

Here are the five signs that warrant an appointment sooner rather than later:

1

Tooth sensitivity when eating or drinking

If a sip of cold water or a bite of ice cream sends a sharp jolt through a tooth, that's your enamel — or more importantly, the layer beneath it called dentine — telling you something is wrong. Sensitivity can indicate early-stage cavities, a cracked tooth, receding gums, or worn enamel. A dentist can identify exactly which tooth is affected and why, and address it before the nerve becomes involved.

2

Bleeding gums when you brush or floss

Healthy gums don't bleed. If yours do — even occasionally — it's one of the most common early signs of gingivitis, the first stage of gum disease. The good news: gingivitis is fully reversible with a professional clean and improved home care. The bad news: if you ignore it, it progresses to periodontitis, which causes irreversible bone loss around the teeth and is a leading cause of adult tooth loss.

3

Persistent bad breath that doesn't improve

Bad breath after a meal heavy in garlic is normal. Bad breath that persists despite brushing, flossing, and using mouthwash is not. Chronic bad breath — known as halitosis — is commonly caused by bacteria in pockets of gum disease, a decaying tooth, or a dry mouth condition. It's rarely a hygiene problem alone, and needs a clinical assessment to find the source.

4

A toothache, throbbing, or pressure in the jaw

Pain of any kind in or around a tooth is a clear signal that something needs attention. Throbbing pain that lingers — especially at night — is often a sign that bacteria have reached the pulp of the tooth and an infection may be developing. Don't manage dental pain with painkillers and hope it resolves. Dental infections can spread and, in rare cases, become dangerous. Same-day or next-day care is strongly recommended.

5

White spots, sores, or unusual patches in the mouth

White spots on teeth can indicate the earliest stage of decay — a demineralised patch that hasn't yet become a cavity. White or red patches inside the mouth, persistent mouth sores that don't heal within two weeks, or any unusual lump or thickening should always be checked without delay. While most are benign, some patches in the mouth warrant investigation to rule out more serious conditions.

The 2-week rule: Any sore, ulcer, or patch in your mouth that hasn't healed within 14 days should be assessed by a dentist or GP. This is a simple, well-established guideline supported by dental associations worldwide.

What happens if you wait?

Dental problems rarely stay the same — they progress. A small cavity that could be filled in one short appointment can, over several months, become deep enough to require root canal treatment. Gum disease that could be reversed with a clean and improved flossing technique can become a chronic infection requiring specialist care.

The other factor is cost. Preventive and early-intervention dental treatment is a fraction of the cost of treatment for advanced problems. A regular check-up every six months is the most financially sound dental decision most people can make.

How often should you have a check-up?

For most adults in good dental health, every six months is the standard recommendation. If you have a history of gum disease, dental implants, or are more cavity-prone, your dentist may recommend more frequent visits — every three to four months.

Children should be seen from the eruption of their first tooth (or by their first birthday) and regularly thereafter.

Don't wait for pain. The majority of dental problems are completely painless in their early stages. A check-up is the only reliable way to find them early.
